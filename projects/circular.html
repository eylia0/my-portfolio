Circular Economy Concepts

A concept study exploring how circular design principles shift products from “use + replace” to “use + maintain + reuse” — across public infrastructure and a consumer product.

Circular Design Concept Study SDG 12

Why circular economy matters here

A circular economy focuses on keeping materials in use for as long as possible — not just by recycling, but by designing products that are easier to maintain, repair, upgrade, and reuse.

In this project, I used circular principles to redesign two very different products: a street light (high-volume infrastructure) and a dolphin torch (everyday consumer product). The goal was the same in both cases: reduce waste by making longevity the default.

  • Design for longevity: extend life through robust structure + easy access
  • Design for maintenance: fast repair to prevent full replacement
  • Design for reuse: give products a second life instead of disposal

What I’m measuring

  • Waste avoidedRepair + reuse first
  • Material reductionLess steel / fewer parts
  • Service lifeDesigned to last longer

Concept 1 — Street Light Redesign

Public infrastructure redesign focused on modularity, lower material use, and simpler maintenance.

Circular strategy

Street lights often fail in a “replace the whole thing” cycle: difficult access, slow repairs, and high labour risk. This concept makes maintenance obvious and fast, so components can be repaired instead of sending the entire product to scrap.

Key design moves

  • Modular access panels: easier servicing of electrical components
  • Repair-friendly assembly: parts can be replaced without removing the whole pole
  • Material efficiency: reduce unnecessary steel through smarter structure
  • Designed end-of-life: parts can be separated for recycling/remanufacture

What improves circularity

By reducing repair time and simplifying access, the design increases the chance that councils maintain fixtures instead of replacing them. That shifts the system from “disposal” to “continued service.”

Concept 2 — Dolphin Torch Improvement

A consumer product improvement proposal: reuse at end-of-life, rechargeable power, and repair through disassembly.

Circular strategy

The torch redesign targets three points in the lifecycle: reuse, resource reduction, and repairability. The aim is to keep the product valuable even when the “torch” function ends.

Design changes

  • Reuse at end-of-life: repurpose as a storage container
  • Resource + waste management: replaceable rechargeable battery with USB-C charging
  • Repair over replacement: full disassembly; parts sold separately to encourage fixing

SDG alignment

These moves directly support SDG 12 through waste reduction, responsible material consumption, and encouraging sustainable practices.

Behavior strategy

The proposal includes a user-facing strategy: packaging + messaging that explains the “2-in-1” value, with a community “Fix It” station idea to make repair normal.

Outcome

Two redesigns showing how circular thinking scales: infrastructure needs modular maintenance systems, while consumer products need emotional and functional reasons to stay in use (reuse + repair + rechargeable design). The unifying idea is simple: design the second life and the repair path first.

Want it even cleaner? Next step is adding 2–3 “impact cards” (e.g., waste avoided, parts replaced, service-life extension) once you confirm the numbers you want to claim publicly.
